Circuit Variables Fundamentals

July 4, 2025 · Circuit Analysis · Electrical Engineering · Mathematics

Circuit analysis is the foundation of electrical engineering. Understanding the basic variables in circuits and their interrelationships is crucial for mastering circuit theory. This article will explore the four fundamental variables in circuits: voltage, current, resistance, and power, along with their mathematical relationships.

Basic Circuit Variables

In circuit analysis, we primarily focus on four fundamental physical quantities. These variables form the foundation of all circuit analysis:

Variable Symbol Unit Description
Voltage $v$ or $V$ Volts (V) Electric potential difference between two points
Current $i$ or $I$ Amperes (A) Amount of electric charge passing through a cross-section per unit time
Resistance $R$ Ohms (Ω) Opposition to the flow of electric current
Power $p$ or $P$ Watts (W) Rate of energy consumption or generation per unit time

Ohm's Law

Ohm's Law is one of the most fundamental and important laws in circuit analysis. It describes the linear relationship between voltage, current, and resistance:

$$v = iR$$

where $v$ is voltage (in volts), $i$ is current (in amperes), and $R$ is resistance (in ohms).

From Ohm's Law, we can derive two other equivalent forms:

$$i = \frac{v}{R}$$
$$R = \frac{v}{i}$$

Power Calculations

Basic Power Formula

Electric power is defined as the rate of energy consumption or generation per unit time. The basic power formula is:

$$p = vi$$

This formula shows that power equals the product of voltage and current.

Alternative Power Expressions

By combining with Ohm's Law, we can derive other expressions for power:

When voltage and resistance are known:

$$p = vi = v \cdot \frac{v}{R} = \frac{v^2}{R}$$

When current and resistance are known:

$$p = vi = (iR) \cdot i = i^2R$$

Therefore, the three expressions for power are:

$$p = vi = \frac{v^2}{R} = i^2R$$

Conductance

Conductance is the reciprocal of resistance, denoted by symbol $G$ with units of siemens (S):

$$G = \frac{1}{R}$$

Using conductance, Ohm's Law can be expressed as:

$$i = Gv$$

Kirchhoff's Laws

Kirchhoff's Current Law (KCL)

Kirchhoff's Current Law, also known as the nodal current law, states that at any instant, the sum of currents flowing into a node equals the sum of currents flowing out of that node.

$$\sum_{k=1}^{n} i_k = 0$$

where currents flowing into the node are considered positive and currents flowing out are considered negative.

Kirchhoff's Voltage Law (KVL)

Kirchhoff's Voltage Law, also known as the loop voltage law, states that in any closed loop, the algebraic sum of all voltages is zero.

$$\sum_{k=1}^{n} v_k = 0$$

Series and Parallel Resistors

Series Resistors

When resistor elements are connected in series, the total resistance equals the sum of individual resistances:

$$R_{total} = R_1 + R_2 + R_3 + \ldots + R_n = \sum_{k=1}^{n} R_k$$

Parallel Resistors

When resistor elements are connected in parallel, the reciprocal of the total resistance equals the sum of the reciprocals of individual resistances:

$$\frac{1}{R_{total}} = \frac{1}{R_1} + \frac{1}{R_2} + \frac{1}{R_3} + \ldots + \frac{1}{R_n} = \sum_{k=1}^{n} \frac{1}{R_k}$$

For the special case of two parallel resistors:

$$R_{total} = \frac{R_1 R_2}{R_1 + R_2}$$

Practical Application Examples

Example 1: Simple Resistor Circuit

Given a $12V$ voltage source connected to a $4Ω$ resistor, calculate:

  1. The current through the resistor
  2. The power consumed by the resistor

Solution:

1. Using Ohm's Law:

$$i = \frac{v}{R} = \frac{12V}{4Ω} = 3A$$

2. Power calculation:

$$p = vi = 12V \times 3A = 36W$$

Alternatively, using another power formula for verification:

$$p = i^2R = (3A)^2 \times 4Ω = 36W$$

Example 2: Parallel Resistors

Two resistors $R_1 = 6Ω$ and $R_2 = 3Ω$ are connected in parallel. Calculate the equivalent resistance.

Solution:

$$R_{eq} = \frac{R_1 R_2}{R_1 + R_2} = \frac{6Ω \times 3Ω}{6Ω + 3Ω} = \frac{18}{9} = 2Ω$$

Conclusion

Understanding circuit variables is the foundation of circuit analysis. By mastering the mathematical relationships between voltage, current, resistance, and power, we can:

  • Analyze both simple and complex circuits
  • Calculate various circuit parameters
  • Design circuits that meet specific requirements
  • Understand energy transfer and conversion in circuits

These fundamental concepts are not only applicable to DC circuits but are equally important in AC circuits, digital circuits, and complex electronic systems. As technology advances and circuits become increasingly complex, these basic mathematical relationships remain the core tools for understanding and analyzing circuit behavior.
